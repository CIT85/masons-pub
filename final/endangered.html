Why Species Become Endangered

Animals become endangered for many reasons, including habitat destruction, climate change, hunting, and pollution. Protecting endangered animals is important because every species plays a role in keeping ecosystems balanced and healthy.

Top 10 Most Endangered Species

Endangered Species Section

Amur Leopard

One of the rarest big cats on Earth, found in the forests of eastern Russia and China. Fewer than 100 remain due to poaching and habitat loss. Conservation programs are now protecting its forest habitat.

Javan Rhino

The Javan Rhino lives only in Indonesia’s Ujung Kulon National Park. With approximately 70 left, it is one of the world’s rarest large mammals. Habitat loss and disease are major threats to its survival.

Sumatran Orangutan

Native to the rainforests of Sumatra, this intelligent ape faces severe threats from deforestation and illegal palm oil plantations. Only about 14,000 remain in the wild.

Vaquita

The Vaquita is the most endangered marine mammal, found only in the Gulf of California. Fewer than 10 remain, and they are often trapped accidentally in illegal fishing nets.

Mountain Gorilla

Mountain Gorillas live in central Africa’s mountain forests. Thanks to strong conservation efforts, their numbers have increased to about 1,000, but they remain vulnerable to disease and habitat loss.

Saola

Known as the “Asian unicorn,” the Saola is one of the rarest mammals on the planet. Fewer than 100 are believed to exist due to hunting and habitat destruction.

Tapanuli Orangutan

Discovered in 2017, this species lives in northern Sumatra. Fewer than 800 survive, threatened by mining and deforestation.

Kakapo

The Kakapo is a large, flightless parrot from New Zealand. With only about 250 left, it’s one of the world’s most closely monitored species.

Hawksbill Sea Turtle

This colorful sea turtle is endangered due to illegal shell trade and coral reef destruction. Around 15,000–20,000 nesting females remain.

Sumatran Tiger

The smallest surviving tiger subspecies, found only on Sumatra. Fewer than 400 remain, threatened by poaching and forest clearing.
